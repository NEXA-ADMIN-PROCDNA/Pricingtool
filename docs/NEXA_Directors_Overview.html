NEXA — Business Development & Pricing Tool

A plain-language guide to how NEXA works: who can see what, how approvals flow, where the app and its data live on AWS, and who manages each piece.

Prepared for ProcDNA leadership  ·  July 2026  ·  Dev environment: https://nexa-dev.auxo.tech

1What NEXA Is

One tool for the whole life of a business opportunity — from first lead to project code.

NEXA tracks every business development opportunity through its full journey: creating the lead, building the pricing (team, rates, hours, costs), getting leadership approval on the pricing, uploading the signed SOW and PO documents, getting the SOW verified, and finally issuing a project code.

Everything that used to live in scattered Excel files and email threads now lives in one place, with a clear record of who did what and when.

2Who Can Log In — and Where Users Come From

The single source of truth for users is Microsoft Entra ID (Azure AD), managed by our IT team.

Nobody creates a username or password inside NEXA. You log in with your normal ProcDNA Microsoft account — the same one you use for Outlook and Teams. This is called Single Sign-On (SSO).

  • IT owns the user list. The company directory (Microsoft Entra ID) is the master record. NEXA reads names, email addresses, job titles, and locations from it — NEXA never invents users.
  • Job title decides your access level. A sync process reads each person's official job title from the directory (e.g. "Partner", "Executive Director", "Senior Engagement Lead") and gives them the matching role in NEXA.
  • Leavers are locked out automatically. When IT disables someone's Microsoft account (e.g. when they leave the firm), they can no longer log in, and the sync also marks them inactive in NEXA.
  • Only people on the list get in. Even with a valid ProcDNA Microsoft account, if you're not in NEXA's approved user list you cannot use the tool.
Why this matters: access follows the HR/IT record. If someone's role changes or they leave, their NEXA access changes with it — no separate cleanup needed inside the tool.

3Hierarchy & Permissions — Who Sees What

Visibility follows seniority. Each level sees its own work plus the levels below it.

ADMIN — sees everything, manages rate cards & exports PARTNER — sees ALL opportunities across the firm │ ED — sees own + Directors' + SELs' opportunities │ DIRECTOR — sees own + SELs' opportunities │ SEL — sees only their own opportunities
  • A SEL (Senior Engagement Lead) works only on their own deals.
  • A Director can see everything the SELs are working on, plus their own.
  • An ED sees the two levels below them.
  • Partners and Admins see the full pipeline.

These rules are enforced on the server — not just hidden in the screen. Even a technically savvy user cannot trick the system into showing them opportunities above their level.

4The Workflow — From Lead to Project Code

Every opportunity moves through fixed stages. Nothing skips a step.

1. Lead created→ 2. Pricing built→ 3. Pricing finalised→ 4. Approval requested→ 5. Approved → SOW stage→ 6. SOW & PO uploaded→ 7. SOW verified→ 8. Project code issued

Stage by stage, in plain words

  1. Lead — the owner (usually a SEL) creates the opportunity: client, expected revenue, dates, line of business.
  2. Pricing — the owner builds one or more pricing versions: which roles, at which rates (from the official rate card), how many hours per week, plus any other costs. The tool calculates billing, cost, and margin automatically.
  3. Finalise — one version is marked as final. Once approved later, this version is locked and can't be quietly edited.
  4. Approval request — the owner picks an approver (a senior leader) and submits. The approver gets an email with Approve / Reject buttons — they can decide straight from their inbox, or inside the tool.
  5. Decision —
    • Approve → moves to the SOW stage.
    • Reject → goes back to the owner with a reason.
    • The owner can also withdraw a pending request (with a reason) if something changed.
  6. SOW & PO — signed contract documents are uploaded and stored safely. A second approval (SOW verification) confirms the paperwork matches what was approved.
  7. Project code — after verification, the opportunity gets the final go-ahead for a project code. This step has a deliberate "are you sure?" confirmation because it's permanent.

Every step — every approval, rejection, comment, and document upload — is logged with the person's name and a timestamp. Nothing is anonymous.

Two sign-offs, always: once for the pricing (before we commit to numbers) and once for the SOW (before we commit to the contract). Neither can be skipped.

5How Data Flows Through the System

A simple picture of what happens when someone uses NEXA.

Your browser→ Microsoft login check→ NEXA server (AWS)→ Database / File store→ Back to your screen
  1. You open NEXA in a browser. If you're not signed in, you're sent to the Microsoft login first.
  2. Every request is checked. The server verifies your identity and role before answering — on every single click, not just at login.
  3. The server does the work. All calculations, permission checks, and record updates happen on the server, inside our AWS environment. Your browser only ever sees what you're allowed to see.
  4. Data is stored in two places: structured records (opportunities, pricing, approvals) live in the database; uploaded documents (SOW/PO files) live in secure file storage. Document links expire after one hour, so they can't be passed around indefinitely.
  5. Emails go out via Microsoft. Approval notifications are sent through our own Microsoft 365, from nexa_admin@procdna.com — no third-party email service touches our data.

6Where NEXA Lives — the AWS Setup

NEXA runs inside ProcDNA's own AWS cloud account (the "Auxo" account) — the same environment that hosts our other internal products.

Earlier, NEXA ran on outside services (Vercel for the app, Supabase for the database). The firm decided everything should live in our own AWS account, where IT controls access, cost, and security. This move is happening in phases:

PhaseWhat movesStatus
Phase 0 — The app itself The NEXA application now runs as a container on AWS (ECS Fargate), reachable at nexa-dev.auxo.tech over a secure (HTTPS) connection. Login and email are confirmed working. LIVE
Phase 1 — The database All records move from Supabase into our own AWS database (RDS PostgreSQL), in a dedicated area (nexa_dev) inside the shared development database server. The structure is already created; the switch-over is next. IN PROGRESS
Phase 2 — Document storage SOW/PO files move from Supabase Storage to our own AWS bucket (nexa-dev-bucket, already created). PLANNED

The building blocks, in simple terms

AWS pieceEveryday-language explanation
ECS Fargate (the server) The computer that runs NEXA. It's "serverless" — AWS provides an isolated slice of computing just for NEXA; we don't maintain any physical or virtual machine ourselves. If it ever crashes, AWS restarts it automatically.
Load balancer (the front door) A dedicated entry point that receives all visitor traffic, checks the app is healthy every few seconds, and forwards requests securely. It holds the HTTPS certificate for nexa-dev.auxo.tech.
RDS (the database) AWS's managed database service. NEXA gets its own separate compartment inside the shared development database, with its own username — other projects can't see NEXA's data and vice versa.
S3 bucket (the filing cabinet) Secure file storage for the uploaded SOW and PO documents. A dedicated bucket, only for NEXA.
Parameter Store (the safe) All passwords, keys, and secrets the app needs are kept encrypted in AWS's secrets vault — never written into the code. Only the NEXA server (and authorised admins) can read them.
ECR + GitHub Actions (the delivery pipeline) When the developer publishes an update, an automated pipeline packages the new version, ships it to AWS, and swaps it in with no manual server work. If a new version fails its health check, the old one keeps running.

Where it sits in the Auxo infrastructure

NEXA runs inside the existing Auxo development cluster (auxo-frontend-dev) that already hosts other internal projects — but with its own isolated service, own front door, own database compartment, and own file bucket. It shares the building; it doesn't share rooms.

Current status: this is the development/trial environment with test data only — a roughly one-month trial before a separate, hardened production rollout.

7Security — the Short Version

Getting in

  • Microsoft SSO only — same protections as company email (including MFA, as set by IT).
  • No separate NEXA passwords for staff to lose or reuse.
  • Sessions expire after 8 hours; you sign in again the next day.
  • Not in the approved user list → no access, even with a valid company account.

While you use it

  • All traffic is encrypted (HTTPS, the padlock in the browser).
  • Role checks happen on the server for every action.
  • Approved pricing versions are locked against editing.
  • Document links self-expire after one hour.

The approval emails

  • Approve/Reject links carry a tamper-proof signed code tied to that specific approver and request.
  • Links expire after 7 days.
  • A confirmation page prevents accidental one-click approvals (and stops email security scanners from "clicking" the link themselves).

Behind the scenes

  • All secrets encrypted in AWS Parameter Store — nothing sensitive in the code.
  • Every approval, rejection, withdrawal, and upload is logged with name and time.
  • Data stays within ProcDNA-controlled systems: our AWS account and our Microsoft 365.

8How the App Talks to Itself — APIs

You don't need the details, but it helps to know the shape.

Everything the screens do — listing opportunities, saving a pricing version, approving a request, uploading a document — happens through about 40 defined "service endpoints" (APIs) on the server. Each one:

  • checks who you are (your Microsoft login),
  • checks what you're allowed to do (your role and ownership),
  • then performs exactly one job (e.g. "approve request", "upload SOW", "add cost line").

The main groups: Opportunities (create, view, update), Pricing (versions, staffing, weekly hours, costs), Approvals (request, approve, reject, withdraw, email actions), Documents (SOW/PO upload & view), Clients (client master and new-client requests), and Admin (rate cards, user list, Excel export to OneDrive).
